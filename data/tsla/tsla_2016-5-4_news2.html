Why Tesla's (TSLA) Earnings Don't Matter

May 04, 2016, 10:04:25 AM EDT By Martin Tillier

Shutterstock photo

In theory, investing should be simple. Public companies are required to give a detailed breakdown of their sales, profits and balance sheet positions every three months, making informed decisions possible. All an investor has to do, then, is to put their money the stock of the most profitable companies. Simple, right?

Of course, if we actually did that we would all just own Apple (AAPL) which is still the most profitable company in the world. What complicates things is that investing is also about predicting the future; something that mankind has had problems with since the days of Nostradamus.

Predicting the future will come to the fore after the market closes today when Tesla Motors (TSLA) report earnings for the first quarter of 2016, or, probably more accurately, reports losses for that period. Tesla is an obvious example of why the “most profitable” theory of investing is fundamentally flawed.

The object of buying stock is to make money and there are plenty of factors other than profitability that can cause a stock’s price to increase. Sometimes it is the tangible; things like advance orders for an upcoming product, or sometimes the intangible; things like hope and vision.

I will freely admit that I have, in the past, tended to ignore the power of those intangible factors. In the case of Tesla I, and many others, have been expecting the market to scream “Show me the money!” at the company for a while now, but despite never having posted an annual profit in GAAP terms the stock refuses to collapse and recovers from every drop.

Even over the last two years as those losses have grown every quarter TSLA has been volatile but has not lost ground overall.

Logically, when you consider the chart and EPS record above you would conclude that something has to give, that TSLA must, at some point soon, come crashing back to Earth. Those that have bet on that and shorted the stock, however, have not done well. The mistake they are making is the same one I have in the past; they are looking logically at measurable, historic things.

TSLA is, instead, all about the immeasurable - the prospect of a cleaner, greener planet, the belief that electric cars are the way forward, the belief that a spectacular product and reinvesting are much more important than eking out a profit now, and the belief that economies of scale and technological advances will one day make the company enormously profitable.

Don’t over analyze - believe!

That is why, as strange as it may seem for a public company, the actual results posted by Tesla for Q1 today matter very little. If anything, history suggests that traders and potential investors should be hoping for a massive miss. That would cause the stock to drop to a good entry point but would be immediately forgotten when the next bit of positive news about the future comes.

If that sounds somewhat cynical, it isn’t intended to be. I actually do believe that Tesla still has the chance to be a truly revolutionary, transformative company and therefore should be a part of investors’ core portfolios.

For the record, Zachs’ consensus estimate for Tesla’s Q1 EPS is -$0.78, an eight cent per share bigger loss than the same quarter last year. It is likely that that will not be the focus this afternoon.

Instead we will be looking at an update on advance orders for the Model 3, a car that has yet to be built. That may seem crazy to some people, but I learned a long time ago that trying to impose logic on financial markets is a losing proposition. It is far better to just accept and go with the flow, and in the case of TSLA that means looking way forward and buying on any dip that comes from something as inconsequential as profitability.
