SolarCity Reduces Installation Guidance Again

Nov. 9.16 | About: Tesla Motors (TSLA)
Bill Maurer
Long/short equity, long only, short only, Growth

Summary

Company now only expects to install 900 MW.

Despite huge revenue beat, non-GAAP EPS barely beat.

Surge in interest rates reduces future cash flow potential.

After the bell on Wednesday, SolarCity (SCTY) announced its third-quarter results via an investor letter. While the company announced a huge revenue beat, it was barely able to transfer that to the bottom line. Additionally, the company reduced its full-year installation guidance, its third such warning this year. As we get ever closer to the vote for the merger with Tesla Motors (NASDAQ:TSLA), investors need to realize what they are getting themselves into.

As this Seeking Alpha headline states, Q3 revenues of $200 million beat by almost $30 million, a tremendous amount, based on higher-than-expected installations. However, non-GAAP losses per share beat by just 2 cents, showing that the company's expense profile still remains questionable. For the year, non-GAAP per share losses are expected to be nearly $10 a share, and that's despite a 3% rise in the share count so far (which spreads out losses over more shares).

The company recorded a nearly $87 million rise in revenues over the prior-year period, and much of that was translated on the gross profit line, an increase of more than $46 million. However, operating costs still plague the company, so the operating loss narrowed by just $5 million despite the huge revenue increase. Throw in interest and other expenses, and the company lost more than $225 million in the period. Through the first nine months of the year, SolarCity has generated revenues of $509 million, but reported a net loss of $759 million. That's not a sustainable business model.

Going into Q3, management was calling for yearly installation guidance of 900 to 1,000 MW. Despite Q3 coming in at 187 MW versus guidance of 170, management took down the forecast to 900 MW for the full year. Remember, the original forecast was for 1,250 MW, so that number has now come down three times. This business is not growing as fast as many hoped, and many believe the future is now bleaker, thanks to a Trump presidency.

Perhaps the worst news of the day wasn't something SolarCity can control, and that is US interest rates. With the company heavily reliant on debt and equity financing, a huge surge in interest rates can heavily reduce the potential future cash flows of this business. As the chart below shows, the 10-year Treasury has seen its yield rise by more than 50 basis points in the past month.

(Source: Yahoo! Finance)

SolarCity detailed a net present value of $4.245 billion from contracts and renewals for its PowerCo portfolio, based on a 6% discount rate. Take that rate up to 8%, and the value drops to just $3.381 billion, a decline of $864 million. That's why the recent surge in interest rates is so troubling, especially for a cash strapped balance sheet that continues to burn through capital (excluding tax equity and other financing deals).

Shares of SolarCity were up slightly in the after-hours session, but they still remain down more than 60% year to date. At this point, shares are moving more in relation to what Tesla's shares do than anything else, so I don't expect these results to massively move the stock. With installation guidance cut again and interest rates soaring, the near-term future of this business just got a bit worse. Investors voting on the deal should consider this warning.
