Nvidia: Stock Has Become ‘A Casino,’ Says Citron, As Targets Soar

Citron Research, noted for its frequent short-selling ideas, argues Nvidia stock has become little more than a gamble on future potential, as price targets rise by the day.

By
Tiernan Ray
Updated June 9, 2017 2:37 p.m. ET
  • Text Size
  • Regular
  • Medium
  • Large
    •
Jim Cramer and Andrew Left of Citron Research discuss Nvidia stock on CNBC's Fast Money: Halftime Report, June 9th, 2017.

Shares of Nvidia (NVDA) are down $12.18, or almost 8%, at $147.76, amidst something of debate on the Street as to whether the stock has become a “casino,” as Andrew Left of Citron Research put it in a segment on CNBC’s “Fast Money: Halftime Report” this afternoon.

The stock has gotten a raft of price target increases in the past 48 hours. Yesterday, it was Citigroup raising its target to $180 from $145, and UBS raising from $148 to $168, and today it’s Argus Research increasing to $175 from $140, and Merrill Lynch raising to $185 from $155.

Argus’s Jim Keller, who has a Buy rating on the shares, writes today that “Nvidia has a clear strategy for successfully monetizing its graphics processing know-how in multiple markets, all of which are fast-growing and transformative."

Keller is not concerned about the surge in the stock this year, as the fundamentals back it up:

For any stock that has risen four-fold in a year, valuation must be carefully scrutinized. Valuation metrics are not getting too stretched, according to our analysis, because NVDA is growing revenue, earnings and cash flow much more rapidly than peers. The two-year forward PEG ratio on forecast FY18 and FY19 earnings is about 3.5-times, which we believe is deservedly higher than the 2.5 average for the peer group of Argus covered semiconductor companies. NVDA is also attractive on forward looking metrics such as discounted free cash flow valuation. We reiterate our BUY rating and 12-month target price of $175 (raised from $140) on NVDA.

Left over at Citron is not convinced, and he spoke with CNBC’s Scott Wapner and Jim Cramer about how the stock has become merely a gamble, despite how much he likes Nevada.

"Two years ago I said don’t look at Ambarella (AMBA), look at Nvidia, and you see how well that worked out,” said Left, defending his prior bullish stance on Nvidia. "But it gets to a point, in a matter of two days, where it’s no longer a stock, it’s a casino."

Cramer countered that the shares at $130 “still have a great deal of optimism."

Left countered, “Does the stock have to go up every single day for it to be a great company?” to which Cramer replied, "I agree with you."

"It’s no longer a stock,” continued Left. “People just got carried away,” adding that Alphabet’s (GOOGL) Google "put out a wonderful white paper on their TPUs,” a competing chip design that could potentially take away some need for Nvidia’s GPUs.

"It’s not Tesla,” added Left, distinguishing Nvidia as a “real company” in contrast to the hype about Tesla Motors (TSLA). "it’s not some dream stock, it’s a real company with real earnings that is going to do real things in the future,” but that still doesn’t merit the current stock price, he said.

Cramer offered that “people are talking about how it’s the next Intel,” a claim that has been made repeatedly by Cramer himself. If one looks at the historical price appreciation of Intel over decades, "That would put Nvidia at $10,500” in share price, he said.

Still, Cramer conceded, "$130 is a lot more realistic a target."
