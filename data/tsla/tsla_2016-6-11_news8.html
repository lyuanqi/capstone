A New Book Argues GAAP Accounting Is Irrelevant

Co-authors Baruch Lev and Feng Gu claim accounting hasn’t kept pace with a changed economy that emphasizes intangible assets.

    Authors Baruch Lev, above, and Feng Gu (below) claim that accounting hasn’t kept pace with a new economy that emphasizes intangible assets like research instead of the industrial economy’s tangible assets like property. Photo: Alberto Cristofari/A3/contrasto/Redux

    Baruch Lev, Israel-born accounting professor at NYU’s Stern School of Business, has long been a skeptic of traditional corporate accounting.

    His contention is that generally accepted accounting principles, or GAAP, have lost their relevance over the last 40 years. The industrial economy, he says, with its emphasis on physical assets of property, plant and equipment, and inventory levels has been supplanted by a new economy in which intangible assets like research and development, information technology, unique business franchises, and powerful brands rule the roost.

    The problem, according to the 77-year- old Lev, is that GAAP hasn’t evolved sufficiently to capture this new reality despite the fact that academic studies show that companies since the 1990s spend more on intangible assets and harder-to-quantify strategic assets—brand development, advertising and marketing, unique personal talents, and the like—than on Rust Belt–like hard assets.

    Enlarge Image Close

    Feng Gu Photo: Courtesy of the University at Buffalo

    Yet corporate R&D, information technology, and other intangible spending must be expensed annually, even though Lev considers these asset investments presumably yielding benefits years down the road. This, of course, punishes current corporate earnings by mismatching costs to eventual, projected revenues. Or as he puts it, the mismatch “contaminates” current earnings.

    “This is why classical GAAP accounting measures like earnings per share, selling, general, and administrative [SG&A] spending, and return on equity now have lost their relevance and predictive value for investors. While R&D spending is separated out on the income statement, there’s not much specificity on how the money is spent, and other strategic spending is buried in SG&A,” he explains.

    The deficiency has only been exacerbated by ever-changing accounting regulations mandating that assets and liabilities on the balance sheet be marked to the market annually and charge-offs taken for any impairments. Restatements now hit earnings statements with increasing rapidity, often subject to management decisions about the best timing. All this leaves investors mightily confused.

    Lev contends: “One doesn’t know, for example, whether a big bath restructuring charge [in which a company takes major, immediate write-downs] betokens trouble ahead for a company or is a measure that will yield further benefits by boosting earnings in future years because disclosure is so deficient.”

    Enlarge Image Close

    Lev has seen fit to write a tome with co-author Feng Gu, a 48-year-old associate professor of accounting at the University at Buffalo School of Management, with the grandiose title, The End of Accounting and the Path Forward for Investors and Managers. In it, the authors delineate how investors can ferret out what constitutes a company’s strategic assets and how successful managers have deployed them to “add value” and maintain a competitive edge within its industry. The book builds on themes Barron’s first discussed with Lev in a cover story nearly 16 years ago (“The New Math,” Nov. 20, 2000).

    IN AN IDEAL WORLD, Lev would like quantifiable strategic intangibles like R&D and information technology spending to be pushed from the income statement to the asset side of the balance sheet where these expenses can be decorously amortized or written down over their profit-enhancing life. This would treat all internally generated technological innovations the same way GAAP treats patents, brands, and technology innovations that companies obtain through acquisition: They would count them as assets on the balance sheet. The current disparate treatment makes no logical sense and is a mockery of earnings comparability between companies that pursue internal versus external innovation strategies.

    Yet after decades of fighting for that reform, Lev concedes that such a change might be a bridge too far for corporate managements and accountants.

    MANY MANAGERS ARE RELUCTANT to give undue visibility and detail about annual promotion spending for a new product or R&D expenses on individual patents. Tying such expenses to projected future revenue streams could lead to embarrassment before company boards and shareholders if the investments don’t pan out. Burying them in the annual income statement can cover up a multitude of failures.

    Likewise, outside accountants worry about litigation risk from shareholder lawsuits, blaming a swoon in a stock price on unexpected asset-impairment charges. The last thing that auditors want is more expenses moved to the balance sheet where accountants must attest to their current value.

    Enlarge Image Close

    Yet Lev and Gu contend that improved disclosure about annual spending on strategic assets would help to even the playing field between ordinary investors and, say, hedge funds, who can afford to drill far deeper into the success or failure of company strategies. After listening to literally hundreds of company conference calls in recent years, they found that almost all analyst questions revolve around companies’ current strategy and success with strategic assets.

    Corporate managements have further muddied the accounting waters by removing all kinds of cost items, including employee stock options, from GAAP earnings to paint a more flattering “pro forma” picture of company earnings power.

    IN THEIR BOOK, Lev and Gu offer four case studies in media-and-entertainment, home and auto insurance, pharmaceutical and biotech, and oil-and-gas industries. For the Internet, insurance and telecom companies in those groups, it’s the strength of the customer franchise that matters, which can be measured by customer growth and brand loyalty (based on customer churn rates). Here quality matters, whether it’s the quality of patents secured, or the star quality of the scientists or entertainers.

    They give big pharma some credit for doing a decent job of disclosing its all-important product pipeline from R&D through the Food and Drug Administration approval stage. Brand maintenance and growth, meanwhile, can be measured by tracking market share trends and successful introduction of new product lines.

    The oil-and-gas industry, Lev avers, is opaque in disclosing its most important strategic metric, company-proven reserves. “These companies constantly buy and sell exploration properties to reshape their portfolio to current economic circumstances and needs, yet they rarely disclose what the consequences of these activities are to the value of their reserves.”

    And, perhaps most important, Lev and Gu contend that one has to try to measure the unmeasurable–the value of the competitive edge that companies boast.

    IN THE ABSENCE of full disclosure of strategic assets by companies and the shortcomings of GAAP accounting, Lev and Gu recommend a do-it-yourself method to measure the impact of intangibles and other strategic assets. They recommend jettisoning GAAP earnings per share in favor of a construct they call “residual cash flow,” which, perhaps, gives investors a truer measure of companies’ prospects.

    To do so, the authors add back such non-cash expenses as depreciation and amortization to reported GAAP net income. They then layer on intangible investments expensed in the income statement, such as spending on R&D, information technology, software, and brands. From the resultant number, normal capital expenditures averaged over three to five years are deducted to remove lumpiness.

    They also reduce the residual cash flow computation with a charge for use of company equity capital that can vary from 7% of net worth for firms in stable industries such as auto insurance to as much as 12% for high-risk biotech companies. Interest expense on debt has already been captured in GAAP net income but not the cost of equity capital. “And one must not forget that equity is not free money and that investors have all manner of alternative investment opportunities,” Lev explains.

    FOR FIRMS MAKING HEAVY investments in intangibles, earnings using Lev and Gu’s residual cash flow methodology come in materially higher than their reported GAAP. For the second quarter of 2013, the Lev-Gu book, for example, pegs SiriusXM (SIRI) earnings on a residual cash flow basis at $230 million, compared with the reported GAAP earnings of $126 million.

Maybe it’s time for a new method that improves transparency of corporate accounting rather than seeking to delude investors.
