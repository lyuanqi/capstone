Tesla's (NASDAQ: TSLA) Fourth Quarter: Red Flags and Red Herrings

Written by Jason Stutman
Posted February 25, 2017

If you’ve caught any of the headlines surrounding Tesla (NASDAQ: TSLA) this week, you might just be under the impression that things are looking up for the much talked-about alternative energy firm.

After all, in a conference call covering Tesla’s fourth-quarter earnings results on Wednesday, Elon Musk made a major announcement: Tesla now plans to build as many as five Gigafactories for the mass production of its electric cars, batteries, and solar cells.

For Tesla’s cult-like fan base and herd of bullish investors, the prospect of three more Gigafactories than what was already on the books means nothing short of massive growth. After over a decade of small-scale production, the company finally seems ready to hit the big time.

Tesla’s upcoming Model 3 is reportedly on track for initial production this coming July, with higher-volume production starting in September. Tesla expects production of 5,000 vehicles per week at some point in the fourth quarter in 2017, ramping up to 10,000 vehicles per week in 2018.

All told, Tesla is aiming to produce 500,000 vehicles a year by 2018 — two years earlier than its original target and a ten-fold increase over the 50,000 vehicles it made in 2015.

At least that’s what Tesla shareholders are banking on...

Tesla Raises Red Flags...

Unfortunately for Tesla bulls, though, the company is no stranger to missing production/delivery targets. Three reporting periods ago, for instance, the company revealed that it missed its target of 16,000 vehicles by 1,180 — a 7% miss, and just the tip of the iceberg.

After a full year of supply chain issues, Tesla has officially missed its initial delivery range of 80,000 to 90,000 cars for 2016. The final number came out to just 76,230.

Tesla has pointed to various supply chain shortages due to its newly implemented technology, which are still a concern moving forward.

As Sam Fiorani of AutoForecast Solutions explained last summer, aluminum, lithium, and other materials, which are already in short supply, could be “limiting factor[s]." These supply shortages are only expected to compound as Tesla scales outward.

And as JPMorgan analyst Ryan Brinkman predicted back in May, the firm “does not believe Tesla will be able to achieve [its production targets] in 2018."

By and large, Wall Street is casting plenty of doubt on Tesla’s ability to meet its ambitious targets. There are plenty of similar analyst notes to those above floating around out there, but even if we put that all aside, Tesla’s future remains dangerously in question.

Perhaps the biggest red flag from this week’s earnings call was the joint announcement that CFO Jason Wheeler will be leaving the company to pursue a position in public policy. This is especially concerning considering that Wheeler joined Tesla in 2015 and, at just 42 years old, isn’t anywhere near retirement age.

Perhaps Wheeler truly does have a burning passion for environmental lobbying, but the sudden career change after just over a year at Tesla is entirely suspicious. A more likely explanation would be that Wheeler didn’t like what he saw on the books and has decided to get out before it’s too late.

After all, during the period, Wheeler reported Tesla's revenue was down by 1% from the third quarter, while operating expenses jumped by a whopping 27%. The firm saw a staggering $151 million in additional operating costs during the period, with $85 million attributable to the recent addition of SolarCity.

At the same time, Tesla’s automotive gross margins fell from 29.4% to 22.6% on a sequential basis, yet there was no major difference in vehicle production between the periods. In fact, Tesla actually produced slightly fewer vehicles in the fourth quarter (24.9k) than in the third (25.2k), and at a lower profit...

Also worth mentioning is the fact that accounts payable/accrued liabilities reached $3.1 billion in the quarter, up from $2.3 billion in the third. Not only is Tesla taking a heavy blow on margins, but the company is shifting even more of its expenses into future earnings reports.

And Red Herrings...

It’s not much of a stretch to say that Tesla’s recent Gigafactories announcement is yet another red herring for the firm. As Fox Business columnist Steve Tobak put it last year following Tesla’s proposal of a SolarCity takeover:

Musk is simply doing what he’s done countless times before: selling investors a bill of goods. To be more specific, presenting some wild, overblown vision of the future so a little bad news in the near-term seems small by comparison. Even his detractors will admit that he excels at that like nobody else.

Tobak’s statement, while not specific to Tesla’s recent quarter, certainly stands up to the times. Musk continues to push the idea of a prosperous future for the company as it burns cash at unprecedented rates (~$450 million of cash consumption in the fourth quarter) and loses money for every vehicle it produces.

At the same time, Tesla is relying heavily on $7,500 tax credits for every car it sells. If those subsidies were to be removed under a Trump presidency (they very well could), it would be an absolute disaster for Tesla and its shareholders. Not to mention, employees are starting to speak out about low pay and poor workplace conditions.

Elon, of course, has been working with the White House in an effort to lobby to Tesla’s benefit, but there’s no guarantee he’ll be successful. Ultimately, the risk is way too high for shareholders right now, especially with a capital raise just around the corner.

Where Tesla Falls Short

At the end of the day, it should be obvious, more than anything else, that Tesla has an efficiency issue. It’s true that the company might be producing some of the world’s most technically advanced products, but if the market isn’t willing to pay the cost, then none of that matters.

And this efficiency issue has only been exacerbated now that SolarCity is under its wing. Not only is the company now adding to Tesla’s net loss, but it's relying on outdated technology for solar energy production.

While it’s certainly possible Tesla may some day adopt this technology itself, right now it’s undeniably out of the loop when it comes to the world’s most advanced solar tech. That’s because one company, much smaller and lesser known than Tesla, is rewriting the book on solar energy at this very moment.

Specifically, this burgeoning firm owns the exclusive rights to a material that more than doubles the efficiency limits of today’s single-layer solar panels. Like Tesla, it’s planning to start initial production later this year and has a number of factories currently in the works.

Of course, there’s plenty of risk that comes along with any investment like this, but at its current size, this firm has remarkably more upside than Tesla, which is arguably valued to perfection at ~$40 billion. Point blank, if you’re a speculator looking for the chance at big returns and are willing to take on the risk, then this is an opportunity you don’t want to overlook.
