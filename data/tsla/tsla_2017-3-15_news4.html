Why Tesla is Gaining After Announcing Stock Sale

By
Ben Levisohn
Updated March 15, 2017 5:12 p.m. ET
  • Text Size
  • Regular
  • Medium
  • Large
    •

Tesla (TSLA) announced after the close that it would issue more stock and sell convertibles. And Tesla, being Tesla, its stock is rising.

Illustration: Tesla/James Lipman

In filings with the the SEC today, Tesla said it would sell $750 million dollars in convertible bonds, and another $750 $250 million in common stock. Now normally, stocks fall when they dilute their shareholders. Not Tesla, though. Its shares have gained 2.7% to $262.55 at 4:46 p.m. today.

Why? It helps that the announcement probably didn't surprise anyone, as analyst after analyst has warned investors about Tesla's cash needs. But there's possible more going on than that.  Convergex's Nicholas Colas issued a note earlier this week looking at unprofitable companies and why they can be good investments. He came up with five potential reasons:

1. Do they reliably hit their operational milestones, both in terms of sales and new product introductions?
2. Do they have a core base of “True believer” shareholders ready to pony up more equity capital when it is needed?
3. What permanent advantages does the company’s business model enjoy? These can be technological or first-mover or network effect related, but they need to be sticky.
4. When the company does hit profitability, what are the operating margins and asset requirements? Do these equate to a return on capital higher than 10% (a notional cost of equity capital)?
5. Is the company being built to operate profitably, or to be sold to a larger entity at some point? Both are viable strategies, but generally you have to choose one and run quickly to that goal

A few of these might describe Tesla, but look at number two. Remind you of anything?

UPDATE: CFRA's Efraim Levy reiterates his Sell rating on Tesla despite the fact that Tesla is trying to "limit dilution of its common shares":

TSLA's planned offering of common stock ($250 million) and convertible senior notes due in 2002 ($750 million aggregate principal amount), with expected gross proceeds of about $1.15 billion, including underwriter options, is on the lower end of our expectations. In addition, the company plans hedging transactions to limit dilution of its common shares. The purpose of the transactions are to improve the balance sheet and support the investments to prepare for the mass-market Tesla Model 3. Lower than expected dilution is a positive, but in our view, risks persist.
