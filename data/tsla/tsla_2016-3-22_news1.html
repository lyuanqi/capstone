Tesla (TSLA) Stock: The Difference Between Investors and Traders

March 22, 2016, 09:34:29 AM EDT By Martin Tillier

Shutterstock photo

Exactly 1 month ago, when Tesla (TSLA) released Q4 2015 earnings that disappointed in many ways, the stock still reacted initially by moving higher. I wrote an article positing that TSLA was different to other stock and that the likely accent on the good news in that report made it a solid buy. Nothing about my long term bullish view of Tesla the company has changed in the last month; there is still phenomenal growth ahead. From a traders perspective, however, TSLA the stock has shifted in that time from a buy to a sell.

I understand that for many people who believe in Elon Musks vision and passionately support Tesla that statement encapsulates what is wrong with the stock market. How can I support the end goals of the company and believe in its long term prospects, yet be prepared to sell the stock? My answer is simple. It has jumped 40 percent in the last month and a retracement of sorts looks inevitable. In that situation, my traders brain sees nothing wrong in selling out now in order to buy more stock at a lower level in the future.

That is one of the most fundamental differences between traders and investors. To an investor, selling a stock on which you have made 40 percent in a month is a crazy thing to do. That 40 percent gives you a nice buffer against any general market downturn and sets you up nicely for a relatively worry free long term hold. Once the model 3s start to flow and the management changes that I highlighted in that article last month start to bear fruit in terms of profitability, any minor correction that may or may not come from here will be an irrelevance.

For a trader, however, not taking some profit on a stock that has risen that far that quickly is the real sin. The way I and other traders see it, if I am right and TSLA retraces from here back to around the $205 support from the end of last year I can increase my holding or be in at a level so low that good profit, rather than just profit, is guaranteed. Its just a matter of simple math.

Lets assume that I bought 100 shares at the opening price of $170.12 on the day that article was published. If I sell them now at $238.32 I realize a profit of $68.20 per share, or $6,820. If I am right and the stock does drop to $205, then that $6,820 of profit allows me to buy an additional 33 shares, increasing my holding by a third. Alternatively I buy the same 100 shares back, leaving me long 100 at an average of just above $100 per share and with an additional $6,820 in the bank.

If I am wrong and TSLA continues to climb, then I simply buy back on a clean break of those end of year highs, at say $250. In that case I have missed out on about $12 of potential profit, or, to put it another way, increased my average price to around $182. The difference in risk to reward in this case is, at least in part, what makes selling here the logical thing for a trader to do.

The other thing that encourages that decision is that TSLAs movement over the last year or two has been dominated by people who think like me. It has become, at least for now, a traders stock. On each move down short interest builds until a squeeze becomes attractive and moves in both directions seem often to end at technical levels. The V shaped pattern evident in the chart above is quite a rare one in stocks and a return to around the starting levels usually indicates a correction of sorts is imminent, and if I can see that, you can bet your life that others can too.

This status of TSLA as a traders plaything is, I am sure, extremely annoying to those that see the long term prospects for the stock. While it lasts, though, I see nothing wrong in trading into an ever increasing position in the stock. When the volatility dies down and the trading stops it will be time to just hold on and enjoy the ride. For now though, with dramatic two way movement, trading rather than investing is irresistible.
