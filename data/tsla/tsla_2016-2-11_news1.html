Why Tesla's (TSLA) Reaction to Earnings Makes it a Buy

February 11, 2016, 09:34:20 AM EDT By Martin Tillier

Shutterstock photo

Some stocks just behave differently, often in defiance of conventional wisdom and even logic. I learned a while ago that Tesla (TSLA) was one of them. If you consider the company to be a car manufacturer, then the fact that yesterday’s Q4 earnings showed a loss of $0.87 per share as opposed to the $0.10 profit that consensus estimates anticipated should have been disastrous.

One can only imagine what would have happened to GM (GM) or Ford (F) if they had posted a similar miss. Instead, though, TSLA soared in the aftermarket. The sock has since given back some of those gains but still looks like opening higher in defiance of the broader market.

There are three things that explain that seemingly illogical response to a widening loss per car and the inability to move to full profitability. Firstly, investors don’t see Tesla as a car manufacturer. They see it as a technology company that makes cars. That difference in mental calculation means that, even in the current risk off atmosphere, the stock is driven by potential rather than profitability. That potential is undeniable. Most people believe that, despite the current glut of oil, the days of gas powered cars are numbered and, so far, Tesla’s electric cars are the best available alternative.

Secondly, from a more technical perspective, a lot of really bad news was already priced into the stock when results were announced after market hours yesterday. I have commented on this phenomenon many times; when a stock is sold off before earnings the downside is naturally limited, and any glimmer of hope in a generally bad report can cause that stock to jump. TSLA had been sold off with a vengeance; at yesterday’s close the stock was down over 40 percent year to date.

 

 

Some of that was the result of a general market collapse and an environment where words like “growth” and “potential” were out of favor and “Show me the money!” had become the phrase du jour. Even so, TSLA’s decline was pretty spectacular and the high short interest (somewhere around 25 percent) indicates that much of it was stock specific. Some kind of a short squeeze was on the cards.

Thirdly, and maybe most importantly, Tesla has Elon Musk and his reputation as a visionary to thank for the stock’s seemingly illogical reaction to a horrible earnings release. Investors seem to be investing in the man rather than the company, so when Musk forecast good things for the rest of the year and deliveries significantly higher than current estimates the stock soared.

It is reasonable, though, to ask oneself whether this time will be any different. We have heard such bullish comments before and so far the recent history of Tesla is one of delays and disappointments. This time, however, could well be different, and not because of Musk.

What convinces me that TSLA is worth the risk at these levels is the new management team beneath him, and in particular the installation of Jason Wheeler, the former Vice President of finance at Google (GOOG). Google, or should I say Alphabet, is a shining example of how a somewhat unwieldy, research oriented tech firm can be transformed from a company with potential to a cash producing machine and it can be assumed that Wheeler learned some valuable lessons in his time there.

Obviously the products are very different but the principles of sound management transcend products. Wheeler was appointed in November, so his influence would not have been apparent in Q4 but as 2016 progresses his focus on making money rather than just headlines should pay off for Tesla. The natural enthusiasm and optimism of TSLA buyers combined with that large short interest will ensure that any hint of those developments will cause the stock to surge again.

Whether or not Tesla can make the transition to a profitable company this year is still an open question, but from a trading perspective that is not the point. The positive reaction to terrible Q4 numbers suggests that in the strange world in which TSLA trades the stock is oversold. At some point soon we will see at least a correction in the broader market, if not a full on recovery and holding Tesla in anticipation of that looks like a smart thing to do.
