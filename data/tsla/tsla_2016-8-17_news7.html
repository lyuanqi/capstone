The Striking Price

Betting on Tesla Motors Falling Back to Earth

Some investors are turning against the heralded electric-car company. Should you follow their lead?

    It’s not easy being an enfant terrible--even when you’re a billionaire. Just ask Elon Musk.

    Alternately regaled and reviled, the founder of Tesla Motors (ticker: TSLA TSLA -0.46071774975751695% Tesla Inc. U.S.: Nasdaq USD328.4 -1.52 -0.46071774975751695% /Date(1500670800468-0500)/ Volume (Delayed 15m) : 4823661 AFTER HOURS USD328.75 0.35 0.10657734470158343% Volume (Delayed 15m) : 77945 P/E Ratio N/A Market Cap 53942981193.4815 Dividend Yield N/A Rev. per Employee 480787 More quote details and news » TSLA in Your Value Your Change Short position ), the electric car company, wants to explore outer space. Lately, he has been getting smashed into the ground. Some investors like his deal to merge Tesla with SolarCity ( SCTY ). Others see it as the marriage of two fancy gimps with shaky financials.

    Musk may ultimately have the last laugh, but for now some prominent investors are turning against him. JPMorgan, for example, is telling clients to bet against Tesla in anticipation that the stock will fall to $170 from its current $224.

    The bank’s trading desk Wednesday told clients that Tesla faces a vortex of deteriorating fundamentals, persistent headline risks, and rising bearish options prices. To pre-position before these forces fully converge, the bank’s derivatives strategists are telling clients to buy Tesla’s January $210 put and to sell the January $170 put.

    The put spread expresses a view that Tesla’s stock will decline some $55 by January expiration. When the stock was at $223.61, the spread cost $13.10. In other words, investors are risking $13.10 to make $26.90. (The net profit is determined like this: $210 less $170 equals the spread, $40. Then, subtract the $13.10 cost of the position to calculate the net profit.)

    The return is attractive, but it is important to understand two key facts. The obvious one is that Tesla’s stock must crater for the trade to reach its maximum profit. Otherwise, this could be an expensive speculation that fails to produce much profit.

    The more subtle fact is that Tesla’s stock and options are pretty much a chaos market. Concerns about the safety of Tesla’s auto-driver functionality, growing operating expenses, and general concerns about sales trends have made the stock a battleground for many bearish professional investors—and, arguably, even more-optimistic individual investors, who like the future described by Musk of electric cars that can largely drive themselves.

    Yet Tesla’s stock short interest is high, and that distorts the put market. When investors can’t easily borrow stock, they buy puts, which function like a short position. When stock prices decline, put prices increase.

    Any quirks in the stock loan market, where shorts operate, tend to drive up the implied volatility of puts. Tesla’s January puts, for example, have an implied volatility of around 50%, compared with about 30% for bullish calls. That means Tesla’s puts are priced with a major fear premium, and that’s why JPMorgan recommends “spreading” the trade versus simply buying one put.

    Tesla shares have ranged from $141.05 to $271.57 over the past 52 weeks. The stock is down some 7% this year even as the broad market has advanced by about the same amount.

    Though the put trade is expensive, the expiration covers several events that could move the stock down to the $170 strike. Also, Tesla’s headline risk is high, which suggests media and analysts could turn any bit of bad news into a big story.

    SolarCity is expected to make a presentation at the Energy Storage North America Conference in October and at Deutsche Bank’s Global Auto Industry Conference in January. It is due to report third-quarter earnings in November. October is typically Tesla’s weakest month. JPMorgan says the stock has fallen an average of 3.6% in October for the past five years. If that’s not enough, the bank notes that Tesla’s controversial auto-drive feature should keep headline risk high.

    “Over the last two weeks alone we’ve seen reports of a new auto-drive accident in China, which comes only months after a fatal accident in Florida, and reports that show TSLA auto-drive sensors can be manipulated with some light radio and sound-emitting tools,” JPMorgan derivatives strategists Bram Kaplan and Marko Kolanovic advised clients in a Wednesday note.

    The bottom line: Tesla’s stock should stay under pressure until January. As the pressure keeps mounting, the fierceness of the stock move will keep rising. If you’re bearish, JPMorgan’s trade gets you into the action at a reasonable price with a potentially solid return.
