Is Amazon.com’s (AMZN) stock available for good price when we account for its growth?

Al Bentley August 30, 2016

Growth expectations for Amazon.com (NasdaqGS:AMZN) are high, but many potential shareholders are starting to ask whether yesterday’s close at $771.29 can still be rationalized by the future potential. I’m going to take a look into that.

Check out our latest analysis on AMZN

What are the future expectations?

Amazon.com potential shareholders have been expecting an uptick in earnings (EPS) for a number of years and if you agree with the 37 analysts covering the stock then in the next 1-2 years we will get to see if they are correct. We should see 195.33% earnings growth and estimates for earnings per share range anywhere from $11.71 to $28.68.



During the same time revenue is predicted to increase from $120,637 M to $232,856 M in 2019 and net income is predicted to catapult from $1,931 M to $11,920 M in 2019, roughly growing 6.2x. Margins are expected to be not high but still acceptable at 5.12% during this time as well.

Is Amazon.com pricey based on current earnings?

Amazon.com is trading at price to earnings (PE) ratio of 187.7x, this also tells us the stock is overvalued based on current earnings compared to the Retailing industry average of 72.6x and overvalued when compared to the US market average of 25.5x .



P/E ratio is simply a stock’s price divided by its earnings per share (EPS). Is a straightforward and popular way of assessing how much investors are willing to pay for each dollar a company earns.

Is AMZN available for a good price when accounting for its growth?

We already know that based on its current earnings it appears rather expensive . But since Amazon.com is a high growth stock, we must also account for its earnings growth by using calculation called PEG ratio.

The PEG ratio (price/earnings to growth ratio) is a valuation metric for determining the relative trade-off between the price of a stock, the earnings generated per share (EPS), and the company’s expected growth. In general, the P/E ratio is higher for a company with a higher growth rate. Thus using just the P/E ratio would make high-growth companies appear pricey relative to others. It is assumed that by dividing the P/E ratio by the earnings growth rate, the resulting ratio is better for comparing companies with different growth rates.

Based on Amazon.com’s predicted 97.1% growth in earnings next year and PE ratio of 187.7x we see that Amazon.com has an acceptable PEG ratio of 1.9x. This tells us that when including its growth in our analysis Amazon.com’s stock can be considered fairly priced based on its fundamentals.
