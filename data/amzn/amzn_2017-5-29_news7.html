Amazon.com, Inc. (NASDAQ:AMZN) Salivating at Trumps Border Adjusted Tax Proposal

By Gene Daniel -
May 29, 2017
SHARE
Facebook
Twitter

Amazon.com, Inc. (NASDAQ:AMZN) has seen its stock rise from $749 at the beginning of the year to the dizzying heights of $995, with the psychological barrier of $1000 within inches – this article looks at how Trump’s Border Adjusted Tax proposal could propel AMZN to territory maybe they didn’t even anticipate reaching in 2017.

With the Trump rally still well on track and the Dow Jones sitting at 21,080, many are questioning where do we go from here. Will Trump’s policies pass through congress? After a shaky start with the first Obamacare replacement attempt being rejected by the House, the Trump administration managed to squeeze through on the second with a 217-213 vote and passing through to the next hurdle of the Senate. This provided much needed momentum to the grandiose plan of transforming the entire U.S. economy and also created more hope that the Border Adjusted Tax does indeed have a chance of passing too.

So what is the Border Adjusted Tax? Many U.S. companies have been using “inversion” to dramatically slash their taxes, a process that involves losing their American citizenship and acquiring foreign companies. The most high profile case of this being Apple Inc. (AAPL) taking advantage of a loophole in Irish law. Trump’s plan is to radically reform tax law in the U.S. to not only discourage U.S. companies from “moving abroad” but actually to reverse the situation entirely with foreign companies investing in American ones, the inversion would become “reversion”. This would come about by not taxing U.S. exports and putting a blanket tax on foreign imports, a bold interventionist move to get money flowing back across to the other side of the Atlantic.

The ramifications are clear for foreign companies with a large U.S. market, since their competitive advantage will most likely be significantly eroded through higher prices and U.S. companies would also benefit from foreign revenues left untaxed.

To understand how this affects a global e-commerce powerhouse such as Amazon, we need to understand its business model.

Amazon.com, Inc. (NASDAQ:AMZN) has many different parts to its business, one is directly selling to its customers with a small markup, keeping inventory in its vast network of warehouses. Amazon also leverages its brand and credibility to create a platform for third party vendors to sell to an ever expanding number of Amazon customers, this reduces risk for the company because it is essentially operating as a middleman picking up sales commissions and operating fees. Approximately half of its revenues comes from this market place and is a massive boon for Amazon because it doesn’t have to worry about the hassle of selling the items but collects extra revenue by providing the Fulfilled by Amazon service – leveraging its infrastructure to package, store and ship items for third party vendors. This marketplace system means that if the Border Adjusted Tax was implemented, the third party vendors would be the ones footing the bill leaving this portion of Amazon’s revenues entirely unaffected.

This cannot be said for Amazon’s some of its competitors however. Since Amazon operates in various different markets, it has too many competitors to count but its brick and mortar counterparts such as BestBuy and Walmart are quivering at the possibility of this law being passed. They are both highly reliant on merchandise from abroad, and this could turn out to be a serious threat to their future.  According to an RBC Capital Markets analysis published in The Wall Street Journal, as much as 85% of Best Buy’s merchandise comes from abroad and 35% for Walmart.

One of Amazon’s biggest e-commerce competitors on the other hand, the behemoth Alibaba, stands to gain from the Border Adjusted Tax. Like Amazon, it operates as a middleman and has positioned itself as the main medium through which the untaxed U.S. exports can go through in order to access the almost 1.4 billion person Chinese market. Donald Trump promised the American people jobs and Jack Ma, the head of Alibaba, has told him he can provide one million of them.

Aside from Alibaba benefiting from the Border Adjusted Tax, Amazon will be looking at this as a chance to establish themselves even further as the leading retail giant by allowing this policy to kill off large amounts of competition without them having to even lift a finger.
