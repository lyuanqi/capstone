Opinion: 5 stocks that should split

By Jeff Reeves

Published: Sept 6, 2016 7:47 a.m. ET

Share

Start with Markel, Priceline, Chipotle, Amazon.com and Alphabet

By

JeffReeves

Columnist

Since it’s back-to-school season, here’s one of my favorite logic puzzles for you. Which weighs more: a ton of feathers or a ton of bricks?

The answer is neither, of course; both weigh a ton even if you would need substantially more feathers than bricks to achieve a weight of 2,000 pounds. But if you don’t stop to think about it, you might get caught.

Investing provides a similar kind of logic puzzle every time a stock splits. Take Church and Dwight Co., CHD, -0.64% which just split 2-for-1 from about $100 to $50 a share, going from approximately 129 million shares to 258 million shares outstanding.

So which version of CHD is worth more? 129 million shares at $100 a pop, or 258 million shares at $50?

Break out a calculator and you’ll see the answer, as with the feathers and bricks, is neither. Both incarnations give us a roughly $12.9 billion personal-care company, even if one scenario has more “pieces” of that company than the other.

Of course, even if it doesn’t change the underlying nature of the company or help CHD sell more Arm & Hammer, many investors love to see stock splits. In their eyes, a split makes a company “cheaper” and allows them to buy more pieces in round lots. A split also smacks of optimism for many investors, since voluntarily moving your share price lower seems to hint at confidence it will keep going higher and you’re just resetting to prepare for the rally.

Unfortunately for those who think this way, Church and Dwight is only the fourth split for large-cap companies in 2016 — the lowest in 20 years.

With markets at all-time highs, that’s not for a lack of potential for splits. There are plenty of good candidates to cut their share price in half, by a third or even 5-to-1. Here are a few of them:

Markel

We’ll start with an off-the-beaten-path name in Markel Corp. MKL, +1.39%   for a few reasons.

For one, Markel boasts a sky-high share price that is one of the biggest on Wall Street — over $900 a pop at current levels. Also, the stock has been on a tear in recent years and should be on your radar anyway.

Shares in this $13 billion insurance underwriter has soared 140% in the last five years versus 85% for the S&P 500 SPX, +0.24%   in the same period, and is up more than 300% from the March 2009 lows. Revenue and earnings aren’t burning down the house this year, but the company is very stable — and with $2 billion in cash on the books and no dividend to speak of yet, we could see a nice income stream from MKL in the near future.

Markel has long re-invested its cash into the business via acquisitions like the $3 billion purchase of reinsurance player Alterra in 2012, but now it’s time to consider a different strategy. A stock split and a juicy dividend could be a great way to re-energize investors.

Priceline

Even pricier than Markel is Priceline Group Inc. PCLN, -0.19%   at about $1,400 a share.

The online travel giant continues to prove naysayers wrong, thanks in part to its strong brand and its ability to fend off competition both at home and abroad. Shares are up 180% in the last five years to more than double the return of the S&P 500 in the same period, though admittedly performance in the last few years of that window hasn’t been as impressive.

That’s in part because Priceline has been a very mixed bag lately, with its May earnings report featuring a big miss and its more recent August report seeing a small miss on the top line. It’s also been painful for Priceline as Wall Street has gotten hip to its perpetual lowball estimates and is no longer “surprised” by modest earnings surprises.

But just like Apple was able to move on from both its super-low forecasts and its super-high share price, Priceline can get past things, too. Last quarter marked a rare moment for the company in that it finally kept in-line guidance, so maybe it’s finally normalizing.

The next step is to normalize its share price as well. A stock split is overdue for this company.

Chipotle

Typically, stocks that have super-high share prices are ones on the way up. But while restaurateur Chipotle Mexican Grill Inc. CMG, +1.01%  has its stock above $400 a share right now, that represents a decline of more than 40% from its 2015 highs.

The reasons should be obvious to anyone watching Chipotle. Food-borne illness scares have sapped customer loyalty and resulted in big declines in traffic, as evidenced by a same-store sales decline of 23.6% reported in its July earnings statement. Furthermore, efforts to revive sales could sap profits without really connecting. An ill-advised move into the crowded burger space and a series of giveaways including free kids meals on Sunday seem good on paper but unlikely to change the company’s unfortunate state of affairs.

For investors, however, it may be time for a bigger change, one that goes beyond coupons. The 150 restaurants opened in 2015 added just 7.5% to its total footprint, and it’s clear the chain is mature if not ubiquitous in the U.S. and must move beyond simply new storefronts.

A stock split may be just a cosmetic thing, but it could help speak to a desire to think differently at the corporate level. If now is not the time for a new perspective, when is?

Amazon.com

You can’t have a list of stocks that need to split without mentioning Amazon.com Inc. AMZN, +0.85% The tech giant is trading for almost $800 a share, and despite the stock being in triple digits since 2009, the company hasn’t shown any desire for a split.

The interesting thing is that Amazon was willing to do three stock splits in three years during the dot-com run-up … but even split-adjusted, Amazon’s share price is now seven times what it was during the tech bubble peak.

But the time is perfect for Amazon to cut down its share price, given a lot of other moves in recent years to play nice with Wall Street and individual investors. Take, for instance, its revelation of Amazon Web Services fundamentals in 2015. Also keep in mind that Amazon has finally proven it can be consistently profitable after years of operating in the red. This kind of corporate maturation is necessary, and some would even say overdue … so the time is ripe for a stock split to go along with these other efforts.

It’s pretty certain that Amazon stock will keep moving higher. As I wrote a few weeks ago, its strong array of products and services from AWS to the Kindle to Prime make it one of the best investments on Wall Street. Splitting the stock would allow more small-time investors to get in on the act.

Alphabet

Some would say Alphabet Inc. GOOG, +0.04% GOOGL, +0.11%   has already split with the debut of two share classes in 2014. However, the tech giant is trading for almost $800 a share with both its GOOG and GOOGL stock, so it’s worth revisiting the topic.

Unlike some of the other companies on this list, there isn’t the same acute need here for some kind of corporate shake-up and a nod toward what Wall Street wants. That mostly came with the rebranding of Google as Alphabet, and the 2015 move to restructure the company into Google advertising and search operations versus an “Other Bets” segment full of ambitious, but nascent, projects.

But like Amazon, there’s something to be said for continuing with this mission via other efforts, including a stock split and, dare I say it, the potential of a dividend like other mature tech companies including Apple Inc. AAPL, +0.89%  and Microsoft Corp. MSFT, -0.17%

Alphabet could use some good headlines as it struggles to out-advertise Facebook Inc. FB, +0.36%   with its digital ad business, out-stream Netflix Inc. NFLX, +0.20%  with YouTube Red and out-AWS Amazon with its own cloud business. This still is one of the most exciting companies on the planet, sure, but it is far from comfortable for the folks in Mountain View right now.

A populist approach to its share price instead of a power-consolidating split like the one in 2014 may be a good way for Alphabet to win some brownie points with current and potential stockholders.
