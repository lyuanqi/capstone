Cramer: There Are Other FANG Stocks Besides These 4

By

Jim Cramer

Follow
 | Jun 28, 2016 | 2:02 PM EDT

On a bounce-back, people can't stay away from FANG. They just can't. And there's a simple reason: growth. These companies have it, others don't. And on a day when the relief rally's in full blossom, Facebook (FB), Amazon (AMZN), Netflix (NFLX) and Google (GOOGL), now Alphabet, lead the charge.

When we look back at what this moment is about, when we search for the ultimate worry about Brexit, it's a recession. The slowdown and uncertainty that this vote triggered turned out to be a lot broader than just the United Kingdom. We aren't able to separate one country's economy from another's, at least when it comes to stocks. Everything's impacted whether we think it makes sense or not because stocks trade in baskets and the baskets have no rationality to speak of.

But some stocks can stand on their own, the so-called super-growth stocks, and people love them just for their bounce-back capabilities.

Let's review why they have such staying power and then I will give you a few more that have similar capabilities and are vying for FANG status.

Right before Brexit, we found out Instagram had just crossed 500 million users. It seems like that was years ago, but it really was just a week ago. That's not a story in itself. What makes it a compelling data point is that it added 100 million users faster than the previous 100 million joined up. Any time you have accelerated revenue growth, that's a home run, and growth investors can't resist even as Facebook gets a huge amount of business overseas. Only half of its business is in the U.S. and Canada and 25% is in Europe. A strong dollar will hurt Facebook, but that didn't seem to matter today.

Amazon, what can I say? First, you get a survey from some outfit called Signal that 42% of U.S. consumers are favoring Amazon as their first choice for holiday gift giving. That's just the kind of thing that comes out with Amazon, some timely terrific survey that gets people excited about it. (Amazon is part of TheStreet's Growth Seeker portfolio.)

But the primary source of excitement came from a press release Amazon issued about its Dash button that allows you to order household products by pressing a button. The button, designed, once again, to allow consumers to skip going to the store, is acceleration mode, the company says, with usage growing four times faster than 2015. There's that accelerated revenue growth component again. Amazon's adding categories galore, with orders occurring twice a minute for popular items including Dial hand soap, Quilted Northern tissue, Play-Doh and Maxwell House coffee. The release includes bullish statements from toy maker Hasbro (HAS) and the beloved Campbell Soup (CPB).

But Amazon always earns its rightful place in FANG with quotes like this gem: "The Amazon Dash Button is a huge success for Trojan Brand condoms," according to Bruce Weiss, vice president of marketing for Trojans. "It literally takes being prepared and protected to a whole new level, making condom buying as easy as pushing a button. The unexpected and fun nature of the leading-edge technology from Amazon," Weiss continues, "goes a long way in bringing condoms more into the mainstream as a normal consumer health product." You betcha. Nerf, toilet paper, condoms, all in a jiff!

When you have a FANG stock, you have a stock that has friends, friends who come out of their foxholes when the shelling stops and say nice things. In a note this morning titled, "Reports of Netflix's Demise Have Been Greatly Exaggerated, Reiterate Buy," research firm Stifel reiterates its buy recommendation and its price target of $143 for the $87 stock.

Stifel says "fears of increased competition in streaming video are overblown," and that Netflix enjoys a sizable scale advantage over competitors Amazon and Hulu with its 81 million global subscribers. Netflix is on pace to release several times more original content than those other guys and Stifel expects meaningful content leverage -- meaning Netflix will make a ton of money off its new shows. Wait, people are very worried that Amazon is spending too much money per programming, how about that? No issues: Content spend, Stifel assures, will grow more slowly than subscribers and investors may be overestimating how much Netflix will need to spend on original programming. Oh, and don't worry about any near-term fluctuations, because it's got exclusive Disney (DIS) programming coming this fall and limited Brexit exposure with only 7% of subs in the U.K!

That's what I love about a FANG stock. There's always someone out there willing to reiterate buy when the shelling ends and this is a quintessential REIT buy, as we call them.

Now the last FANG stock, Google, has become the problematic initial. We got a REIT buy from Morgan Stanley, saying the selloff has been overdone. That's OK. But this is a half-baked push because it goes on to trim numbers, which is deadly, on decelerating paid search revenues, which is fatal even as the analyst keeps it as a buy with an $865 target. Sheesh, with backers like that, a stock doesn't need enemies.

I like Alphabet, my charitable trust owns it, because it sells at only 20x earnings and it remains a household name with a huge cash position and lots of opportunities. But it has a gigantic foreign earnings stream and we have heard this slowdown talk endlessly. Not to worry, the note tells us, "Innovation-Based Drivers Still Intact." I agree, but no FANG stock can ever handle an actual estimate cut.

Which brings me to an interesting juncture. The darned alliteration has been the thing keeping Google in FANG. But it's time to recognize that even though I think the future's incredibly bright for this one, when it comes to bounce-back candidates, it's been overtaken by a couple of other names.

First on the list is Broadcom (AVGO), the communications semiconductor company that's the survivor of the Broadcom-Avago merger. This stock is a total no-quit situation, and right on cue, Morgan Stanley reiterates its buy, noting, "The stock's valuation multiple of 12 times earnings does not fully reflect sustainably high gross margins of 60% and healthy end-market diversification, meaning it's not totally dependent upon Apple (AAPL) or cellphones in general. Those kinds of notes make you want to make FANG into FAN-B, but what the heck does that mean? (Facebook, Google and Apple are part of TheStreet's Action Alerts PLUS portfolio.)

The second? Ulta (ULTA). Here's the non-Amazonable, fast-growing cosmetics and hair salon company that truly is riding the wave of cosmetic necessity caused by high-resolution cellphones. In a world where Apple's stock can't get out of its own way even when Cowen notes that it is in a supercycle, Ulta has become the de-facto domestic name that growth investors gravitate to. No one reiterated its buy recommendation today. Didn't need to, darned thing didn't sell off! Hmm, maybe it's the vowel we need to buy to create a five-letter FANG replacement without Google. Let's play Jumble: FUBAN, UNFAB? You tell me.

Anyway, be aware that this is ultimately a game with no end. These stocks will always bounce back, but the issue is from what level. We can't ignore Brexit, we can't ignore a slowing world, and while all of these stocks have high growth potential, nothing's immune in the end if we get a real recession except companies like Clorox (CLX) and Altria (MO), the kinds of staples that never need to be reiterated as buys because they are the stocks that shine in chaos. Today, with a chaos respite, they sell off. Don't worry, if Royal Bank of Scotland (RBS) or Lloyds or Barclays (BCS) or some other British institution hits the skids again, they, and not UNFAB, will be back making all-time highs, as they always seem to do.
